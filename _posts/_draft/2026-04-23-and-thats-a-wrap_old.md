When we sailed into Mahurangi on Thursday 2nd April we crossed our own track from before Christmas, closing the loop on our New Zealand adventures — and a circumnavigation of the North Island. The end of a chapter.

We had hoped to make it around the South Island too, but that was always an ambition rather than a plan, and ultimately our priority was simpler: relax, enjoy ourselves, and get back into life out on the water after our year in the marina. We're proud to report we've succeeded (ok, some of it wasn't relaxing as you'll see below, but other than that!).

![Our track encircling the North Island]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/posts/nz-track.webp)

## New Zealand Weather

We had, naively perhaps despite the stories, anticipated an easier summer cruise than the New Zealand weather offered us. Our overriding experience of the sailing, and a caution to anyone unfamiliar with these waters, is that New Zealand experiences weather. Lots of it!

My memory of the route over these four months is firmly tied to the weather and regular strong winds that we had to plan for and find appropriate shelter from. This broadly went as follows:

- **Christmas in Kawau**, sheltering from 30 knots.
- **Bay of Islands before New Year** — made it into Opua marina just ahead of forecast 40+ knots.
- **Golden Bay**: the final 50 miles of our passage to the South Island were upwind in 20–25 knots. We then stayed put for a week off Tata Beach waiting for 30 knots gusting 50+ around Separation Point to abate, watching spindrift blowing through the anchorage. Also, it rained *a lot*.
- **Nelson**: anchored in the river outside the marina, a day of 30 gusting 40+ knots, perpendicular to a 3-knot tide, with 50 knots reported in the marina itself — the windiest we've experienced at anchor anywhere.
- **Marlborough Sounds**: hid several times — standard practice, apparently, with Cook Strait doing its thing. On one occasion, deep in Pelorus Sound, 70 knots was reported off Wellington and 13-metre waves. We'll take the hiding, thank you.
- **Lyttelton**: beaten in by a Southerly, fortunately only by an hour this time. Cold, wet, and followed by a surreal mayday event on arrival — a day boat lost on the rocks in the bay we anchored in - we took to the marina for a week and jumped ship to stay with the cousins.
- **Great Barrier Island**: served up a two-day storm you may have [already read about]({{ site.baseurl }}/2026/03/26/anchoring-out-a-storm-in-kiriwiki-great-barrier-island). When it rains here, it rains - something like 150mm rain with this one (>5in).
- **Back in Whangarei**: Just to top it off, our first weekend after hauling out on our return to Whangarei saw a tropical cyclone pass close by. We took up friends (thanks James and Steph!) on their kind offer of a bed for this one.

> New Zealand's summer was shaped by an "extremely active pattern across the country", with lower than normal air pressure and a weak to moderate La Niña helping drive months of volatile weather. This setup fuelled severe storms, record-breaking rainfall and destructive winds, affecting communities from Northland to Canterbury.

## The Bits Between The Weather

To focus only on the weather would be to totally miss the point. Cruising New Zealand has been immensely enjoyable, rewarding and, yes, mostly relaxing. We sailed down the West Coast of the North Island in one push and spent a couple of months working through the South Island — Abel Tasman, the Marlborough Sounds, across to Lyttelton and as far south as Christchurch — before turning around and cruising back up the East Coast, stopping in Gisborne, Tauranga, various islands off the Coromandel and finally making it to Great Barrier Island on our way back into Whangarei.

We have had so many amazing experiences, seen some spectacular coastlines, wildlife and landscapes (as you would expect from New Zealand) and also enjoyed some lovely warm summer weather. We have sailed around some of New Zealand's intimidating corners and headlands - Cape Reinga (top of the North Island), Cape Stephens (top of D'Urville Island, above the Marlborough Sounds), Cape Jackson (corner of the Marborough Sounds, edge of the Cook Straight), and East Cape (Eastern most point of the North Island) - all in benign conditions with the sun shining. I'm sure there was at least as much luck in that as there was judgement!

## Preparing For Departure

Sailing back into Whangarei was a surreal experience, we are firmly home from home coming back into the Town Basin. When we leave this time, very soon now, it will be for good and is going to be a tough one for us. Fortunately we are all very excited about getting back to the tropics and experiencing everything that Fiji and Vanuatu have to offer, and who knows what beyond...? Trade winds, hopefully!!

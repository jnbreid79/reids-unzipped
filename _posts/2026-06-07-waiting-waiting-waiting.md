We've come to the end of the first week in June and you might have noticed that we're still in New Zealand. Not great progress to Fiji, we had certainly hoped to be there by now. This wasn't the plan; it's getting cold in New Zealand again!

> Sunday 7th June; Go, or No Go? Sadly it's yet another No Go.

Since we finished getting the boat ready in Whangarei in the first week of May, with Bumpy onboard ready to go, there have been four or five potential weather windows. Some of these were a bit tenuous, but we've been quite excited about a couple of them. Sadly, the forecast for each has consistently deteriorated as the departure date has approached. True to form, we cancelled our 1pm scheduled check-out appointment with Customs this morning after reviewing the latest weather routing. This has been the hardest one; making the decision itself is really difficult and we're always full of anxiety, but also, this one had been looking the best for so long. Up until a couple of days ago.

We (well Jamie and this chap called Claude who's still proving very helpful) have been developing our own basic app to help track the evolution of forecasts each time new ones come out. Firstly this saves time when a new forecast arrives (a quick yes, that's similar to last time means we don't have to bother looking in more detail) and secondly to help remember what it was we were looking at last time when we think it now looks a bit different; we believe this is a major gap in all the main weather routing solutions. How forecast conditions evolve through time is at least as important as what the currently forecast conditions actually are. The comfort measure in the below is the one derived in OpenCPN's weather routing and is quite rudimentary, but nevertheless has been helpful, and does show the deteriation in the forecasts this week (each new forecast is a new row on the y-axis).
![Forecast deteriorating this week]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/posts/forecast-evolution.webp)


There are lots of reasons we could have chosen to leave for Fiji this morning:
- This is very likely a feasible weather window
- Wind and waves are behind or on the beam all the way
- There may not be a better opportunity in the future
- It's getting late; winter is arriving in NZ
- We've got to go at some point, right?!

The decision not to go is eating us up. The anxiety in making the decision is one thing, followed by the 'what-if' that comes in the days afterwards. Maybe it would be fine, maybe we'd get through the passage ok and after a few days or so probably forget about it, or at least move on and be happy we're back in the tropics anyway. 

However, we still have a choice, we are not under any obligation, other than our own schedule and desires, to go to sea. There are still (in our opinion) better reasons than those above to say "No, this isn't the window for us". 

> We don't want to make an elective choice to go to sea, with the family onboard, on a 7–8 day passage when the passage is going to be a rough one ("salty" or "sporty" depending on which weather router you listen to!) and there is no bail-out option nor contingency for sailing slowly.

## The main factors

**It is going to be windy.**
Although not upwind, the forecast suggests we'd see wind gusting over 30 knots for the whole second half of the passage. Not far behind the beam, this would be a very lively and powerful beam reach. We try not to sail in gale-force winds.

**The waves are going to be big.**
A system to the east of New Zealand is forecast to push very large waves up the course. We'd be likely to experience 5m waves at a period of 12 seconds. Our rule of thumb is that we like to see the wave period in seconds more than three times the wave height in metres. Although the waves are not from ahead (so we're not going to be leaping off them), they are going to be steep and likely have Zipper surfing at speed into the wave in front. This loads everything; the autopilot, the rudders, the rig, the structure of the boat, not to mention the anxiety of the people in the boat.

![Big waves mid-passage]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/posts/mid-passage-waves.webp)

**There is no reasonable bail-out option.**
We had been hoping that Minerva Reef — a spectacular coral reef in the middle of nowhere that we'd love to stop at given the opportunity — would offer sanctuary if a detour or delay were required. However, with these size swells it wouldn't be safe even if you could overcome the possible 8-knot outflowing current into the wind through the pass, which we definitely couldn't.

**There is no contingency for a slow passage.**
The GFS model has a system forming in the vicinity of Fiji around the time we are scheduled to arrive. A slower passage by even a day would put this system directly on our route, with dangerously strong winds. Maybe the system won't form as forecast and in all probability it won't, but it's certainly a risk. In our experience of long passages it is often the case that we choose to really slow the boat down, either managing an issue that's developed on the boat or just for crew comfort. We don't want to be in a position where we have to keep up a relatively fast speed for a week to avoid being run over by a low pressure system.

![GFS model showing possible system forming near Fiji]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/posts/possible-system-forming.webp)

Incidentally, the squash zone driving the strong easterly winds on the approach to Fiji 
is a result, according to our basic meteorological understanding, of the latest BFH (Big
 Fat High). Jim on *Roam* wrote an excellent article [about these in May]
(https://www.sailroam.com/new-zealand-to-fiji-passage-planning/) when we all turned down
 a previous window (although this one isn't _quite_ as big as that one was).

## Flexibility

Staying behind again, for who knows how long this time, has made us rethink what we're trying to do. It seems that the winds are trying to tell us we should be sailing further west. After all, the weather is largely in charge of when we can sail and where we can sail to. Maybe we should just pay more attention to that and go with the wind?

Putting on our flexibility hats, it's apparent that we could be setting off on a much more reasonable-looking passage if we were sailing to New Caledonia, further to the west than Fiji. From there it would be a short hop up to Vanuatu, so can we just do that instead? Well, yes, having spent a day thinking about it we think we can. It's not as if New Caledonia isn't also a wonderful place to explore. So we'll just switch it round: instead of cruising Fiji and missing out on New Cal, we'll go to New Cal and miss out on Fiji. After all, we can't see everywhere. We'll have to come back again some other time — maybe?!

## New Caledonia

This afternoon we have booked a new departure meeting with Customs, for 8:30am on Tuesday. Our plan is to head north on the initially southerly winds out of New Zealand, and bend around to the west as the wind backs into the east. Of course by delaying until Tuesday there's a chance the forecasts will once again ruin our plans, but in the meantime we're getting ready and crossing our fingers.

> Get ready New Caledonia, Zipper incoming! (Maybe!)

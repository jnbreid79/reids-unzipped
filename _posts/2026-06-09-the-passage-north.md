After so much waiting for the right weather window,and a lot of um'ing and ah'ing, we made the decision to set sail for New Caledonia instead of Fiji. We took a day to make sure this wasn't just a knee-jerk reaction and would be a decision we'd be happy with in the weeks to come, then got the boat and ourselves ready for the passage and set off on Tuesday 9th June.

![Completing the formalities for customs when checking out in Opua]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/gallery/2026-06/completing-the-formalities-for-customs-when-checki.webp)
![The crew for the passage north]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/gallery/2026-06/the-crew-for-the-passage-north.webp)

We visited customs to complete the requisite forms, and when Isla had added Zipper to their whiteboard (alongside our friends Tempus, who'd left the day before) we were ready to set sail North.

## The Plan

The forecast was for a largely downwind passage, the wind starting just west of south and gradually backing all the way round to east-south-east before our anticipated arrival, starting off strong, dropping in the middle, before picking up again at the end.

We took heed of the sound advice coming back from other boats sailing north earlier in the month:

> If you expect the gusts to be 20% stronger than forecast and the direction to be 10 degrees worse, you'll get what you expect.

With that in mind, the last day was looking quite aggressive if we sailed the direct route; we could expect winds well over 30 knots on the beam, with 3.5m swells also on the beam. Further, our experience sailing around Cape Reinga in January told us that the seas there are uncomfortable at best even in benign conditions; it gets very confused where the oceans meet at the top of New Zealand. So we didn't want to get too far West too soon.

Our route was therefore a bit of an arc, heading north to start with and ending up with more westing towards the end as the wind backed.

## On Passage

![Sunset on the first night of the passage]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/gallery/2026-06/sunset-on-first-night-of-the-passage.webp)
![Lego on one of the calmer days on passage]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/gallery/2026-06/lego-on-one-of-the-calmer-days-on-passage.webp)

The first couple of days were lively and we were grateful to have Bumpy onboard with his wealth of knowledge and experience and his willingness and ability to contribute to all aspects of boat life. Another opinion or pair of eyes is always appreciated.

We had a very quiet night in the middle where the wind really did drop more than we'd anticipated. This allowed for some lego and reading after the fast and furious start to the passage. But the calm wasn't to last, although the children carried on playing and reading as the wind and waves picked up. Other than the brief respite, we had exactly what we'd taught ourselves to expect from the gribs.

On our last night approaching New Caledonia we were very grateful for our easting. We were able to sail a very deep angle with the wind gusting into the 30s and generally feeling a bit on the edge. With three reefs in we were surfing down steep swells in the dark, regularly hitting 15 knots boat speed before skewing round at the end of each surf.

![Pretending all is calm despite the increasing swell]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/gallery/2026-06/pretending-all-is-calm-despite-the-increasing-swel.webp)
![Some passage statistics on the chart plotter]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/gallery/2026-06/passage-stats.webp)

In hindsight we should have dropped the main completely, and on an equivalent forecast we will do so in future. When our biggest surf went over 20 knots — breaking our all-time speed record — and gust exceeded 35 we decided we needed to do something. Heading up in these conditions to drop the main would have been lively to say the least, so we opted instead to drop the jib. Although still very fast, this brought things back under control and dropped the anxiety levels to manageable for the rest of the night.

## Arriving at the Boulari Pass

We timed our arrival at the Boulari pass, on New Caledonia's western reef, perfectly for daybreak. As we rounded the south-eastern corner of the reef we had to head up slightly to sail parallel to it, before really turning up into the wind to come through the pass — which is angled north-east. The wind dropped with daybreak and the swells reduced along the side of the reef, so the close-hauled course to get through wasn't too uncomfortable. We did it without the jib and with both engines running, just to be sure. The leading lights guided us in through the northernmost of the three passes, which we chose because it was the most northerly angle and therefore less into the wind than the wider central pass.

In the event we navigated the pass simply enough and made it round the islands inside onto our heading up to Nouméa. Phew.

It felt like time to draw breath, but no sooner had we done so than we were enveloped by a squall - suddenly we had absolutely torrential rain that flattened the water with 30 knots blowing it sideways. Fortunately we were already reefed right down, but now we were charging into the atoll at high speed with visibility no more than 50 yards. Having not been here before this was rather alarming, but fortunately it was around 10 miles to the next turning point and the squall had passed by the time we got there. We were banding around thoughts of reaching back and forth between the pass and the town until it cleared if we had to!

In looking back at the log we sailed 209 miles in the 24 hours up to the edge of the New Caledonia reef, breaking our all-time 24 hour distance record that we had set on the way down to New Zealand. We sailed 924 miles and docked in Noumea an hour short of 5 days after leaving Opua.

> New Zipper records:
 - 209 miles in 24 hours
 - 20.6 knots boat speed

## One Last Surprise

Of course, no proper passage is complete without the compulsory engine or other failure just as you're coming into port, and this time was no different.

![The steering sensor issue]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/gallery/2026-06/steering-sensor-issue.webp)

The rudder sensor failed just as we dropped the main. Hand steering seemed to work, fortunately, but I jumped into the engine room nonetheless to see if I could spot anything obvious. The answer was yes: the sensor pin had simply fallen out of the sensor arm — a simple fix to just push it back in. However it then became apparent what had actually gone wrong. The steering cylinder had rotated such that the bleed nipples were now pointing up, and in this orientation the right-hand one was butting up against the sensor arm when steering to starboard. Easy enough to rotate the cylinder back down, but it was clear we'd been rather lucky not to suffer a much more catastrophic failure in the hydraulic system as a result. These things normally happen at the worst possible time, but here — with the autopilot having worked faultlessly for the previous five days — lady luck was shining down on us. I dread to think what would have happened if this had let go in the big weather on a high speed surf in the night!

Problem fixed and mooring complete, the only thing left was to find the baguettes and croissants. You wouldn't get those in Fiji.


## The Preparation

We were a little out of practice, but regardless of how many times we've done it we always seem to suffer from pre-passage nerves. Is the forecast right, what if that system forms a little to the South and/or a little earlier, who's going to be seasick and how badly, what's going to break this time, what have we forgotten? Of course most of this, other than continuing to keep the boat well maintained, we can't do anything about - so the nerves are a consequence of the uncertainty inherent in this way of life as much as anything. But there is a lot we can do to make sure we are as prepared as we can be each time we venture far offshore. 

The day before departure is often a full day of cooking. Sophie has become an expert in making sure we don't need to cook early in a passage (noone wants to cook on the first day or two) nor later on if it's going to be rough. We set off this time with food prepared for most of the passage, and indeed other than cooking once on the calmer day in the middle we didn't need to.

Seasickness is something that we all suffered from at times sailing around New Zealand, in fact Angus, who never suffered at all between France and New Zealand, has had it frequently since we left the pontoon in December. Thinking we had nothing to lose Sophie researched some exercises to try and condition us to the sensations that cause motion sickness; a conflict between what your eyes and your balance are telling your brain. So we all participated, Bumpy included, for the three or four weeks before leaving in 15 minutes a day of various head movements with eyes periodically open and closed whilst tring to balance on a soft surface (occassionally made impossibly hard in rockier anchorages!). 

> Eyes closed. Look up, look down, look right, look left, tip the water out one way, tip the water out the other way, ... 
>
> The crew running through our sea sickness exercises
> {: .cite}

We also work hard to manage our activities early on in a passage to minimise the risk of seasickness - keep in the cockpit (or lying down below), no reading, certainly no screens, minimise cooking, etc etc. I guess we'll never know if it was the exercises that made the difference, but, this was a rough passage at times including a fast start and all five of us were fine for the whole passage. Do get in touch if you'd like to know a bit more about this, we'll certainly be doing it again ahead of future passages.

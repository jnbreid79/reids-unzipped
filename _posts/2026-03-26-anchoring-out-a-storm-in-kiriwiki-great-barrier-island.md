I'm writing this at midnight, anchored in Kiriwiki Bay on Great Barrier Island, with a storm raging outside.

In front of me I have the nav station, primarily telling me the current wind strength and direction and our depth; an iPad with our iSailor app fully zoomed into the anchor alarm circle that marks the perimeter of our swing, showing me exactly where we are by GPS (importantly, relative to the track of where we've been since we anchored here a couple of days ago - see the lower screenshot); and an iPhone showing PredictWind reports of conditions nearby — primarily from other boats in the anchorage but also weather stations up and down the coast. These are the ones I'm looking at:

![PredictWind boats data showing conditions in the anchorage]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/posts/pwdata-kiriwiki.webp)

> *Is it just us, are we in a bad spot, or does everyone else have the same? 
Is it getting worse or abating?
How much longer does it look like it'll last for?*

As I ponder whether I'd actually *do* anything based on the answer to any of those question, I realise I really should be going to bed. After all, the wind strength is mostly in the mid-teens and regularly even in single figures. However, every few minutes it's suddenly in the mid-high-20s or over 30 knots, the 'windy' noise reverberating around the boat as the mast, rigging and everything else is keen to tell us we've just been hit by another gust. Tonight even the port jackstay is getting in on the action, jumping up on top of the cabin roof and slapping it like there's something very heavy jumping around up there.

These gusts often have very little bearing on the wind speed the nav station is telling me — some really big ones come with apparently only 15 knots showing, whereas at other times it's reading high-20s when it feels and sounds like there's nothing going on out there. A sure sign of turbulence: what's happening at the top of the mast is clearly not the same as what's happening down here on the surface. That's often what happens when you anchor in a "protected" spot like this behind a big hill — but this is a small price to pay in avoiding the consistent 40–50+ knots that the nearby weather stations have been reporting up and down the coast all day (see Mokohinau Island to our North and Channel Island to our South in the wind report, above).

It's also pouring with rain — and has been for more than 24 hours now (update: in less than two days we had 134mm, >5in) — adding to the general clamour and making it particularly unappealing to even crack the cabin door open, let alone venture outside to see what's really going on out there. Everything inside is wet and damp regardless, so perhaps it wouldn't make much difference? No, it would, it's not *that* wet inside.

> *There's a disconcerting kind of clunking noise that's started up this evening, every time we snatch back hard on the chain. 
*Hold on, here's another — only 20kn apparently, is that track on the iPad very slightly further back? Maybe not, maybe it's fine and just the same as the rest after all. Depth looks fine too, so if we did drag it can't have been far.*
![Anchor alarm on iPad showing our position relative to the anchor]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/posts/anchor-alarm-kiriwiki.webp)
I really want to know what that noise is, but not enough to get absolutely soaked in the process right now. Hopefully that's a decision I won't regret.*

## Why am I watching screens at midnight?

So why am I here looking at all these screens — what does that actually achieve? Almost certainly, nothing. Assuming we do hold through the night, why did I not get a good sleep and feel fine about it all tomorrow when the wind's abated (which it really should be doing by dawn)?

Well, a couple of reasons.

**Firstly, we've watched at least five boats move today.** In these conditions, boats moving around an anchorage isn't a voluntary change of scenery — that's people who have dragged anchor or have become particularly uncomfortable where they are. It seems tonight is our turn to be unsettled, and to tell the truth I'm a bit upset about that as the general picture is that the wind has been dropping since lunchtime. We sat through last night and all morning today in relative peace, strangely sitting to the north of our anchor with a full gale blowing from the north-east. We felt a bit smug about our spot — OK, complete luck and no skill involved; there just happened to be a nice space here when we arrived, and we haven't been in here before, so we didn't know it was going to turn out to be one of the better positions — compared to the wind data we were seeing from other yachts nearby. Although with friends on some of those boats, not too smug; we were anxious for them too. Foredeck lights going on, people on deck, anchors being reset through the night.

This is a healthy reminder that there are times when *doing* something is absolutely the right option. Clearly we weren't relaxed enough to be fast asleep last night either, but we were definitely more comfortable than we are now. As the wind has gradually backed further into the north, our spot seems to be becoming less and less sheltered. So the decision to be made is: are we still happy here, do we think we're safe, or do we need to move? Right now, yes, we're safe. We're sitting to the south of our anchor, which in some ways is actually more reassuring — aligned to the actual wind direction. More importantly, if we were to drag, we've got a bit more space behind us in this direction; the other way we had not much more than 20 metres before we'd have hit the shore. But how about in another hour, or an hour after that? Maybe I'd better keep an eye on it, just in case.

**Secondly, if something were to happen, it would likely happen fast.** Sure, an anchor drag is often a relatively slow affair, and hopefully our alarms would go off in plenty of time to react. But what if something more catastrophic happened — if something failed and we just let go of the chain, the anchor, the ground — then we'd be off downwind in a hurry. Recovering from that when fast asleep feels unlikely. The experience of waking up to the screeching sound of metal that night in Tonga (sorry again, Joe!) makes it hard to relax. Hopefully that scar will heal one day (I had planned to write a bit about that, but not got round to it). The anchor alarms are not going in the oven again anyway (which is what happened that night), even if there was lightning around.

> *Oh no — genuinely less than 5 minutes after writing that, there's a flash. We have lightning here now too.*

## My options

So tonight the options are realistically:

1. Lying down below, kept awake by the weather, worrying whether we are still in the same place
2. Sitting up here, seeing that we are still in the same place, wanting to fall asleep

Neither is relaxing. Certainly neither actually involves any sleep. But option 2 feels slightly better for the nerves than option 1. And, whilst I'm up I might as well write a bit about it, so here you go.

The more we anchor out storms — and this summer in New Zealand there have been a few, a topic for another day — the more we wonder: 

> Imagine finding somewhere to live where you can go to bed at night and not worry that it might move and crash into your neighbour, or a rock and tip you out into the sea, or that your neighbour might crash into you? Sounds wonderfully relaxing. Maybe even with a fireplace to keep you warm and dry. Surely someone's thought of something like that… anyone?!
